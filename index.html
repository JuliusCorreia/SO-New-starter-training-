1 Aviation security framework ▶

1.1 Threat concept – MITC

Threat is a combination of:

  • Motivation – why someone wants to act.
  • Intent – how determined they are to act.
  • Capability – what they are able to do.
  • Threat – the overall outcome when the first three are present together.

If Motivation, Intent or Capability increases, overall Threat increases.

Mnemonic: “My Intent Creates Threat” → Motivation, Intent, Capability, Threat.

1.2 DfT, CAA and Heathrow

DfT – Department for Transport

  • Government department and policy-maker for transport and aviation.
  • Sets strategic aviation security policy and national threat levels.
  • Accountable to Government and Parliament.

CAA – Civil Aviation Authority

  • Independent regulator for UK aviation.
  • Regulates safety and security, oversees compliance, audits and enforcement.
  • Protects passengers and consumers.

Heathrow

  • Airport operator.
  • Runs day-to-day operations.
  • Implements security procedures and local SOPs in line with DfT/CAA requirements.

Similarities and differences

Aspect DfT CAA Heathrow
Role Policy and legislation Regulation and oversight Front-line delivery
Focus National framework, threat levels Compliance, certification, quality assurance Local SOPs, staff training, daily running
Accountability Government and Parliament DfT and legislation CAA, DfT, owners, airlines

The Single Consolidated Direction (SCD) sits within the National Aviation Security Programme (NASP) and sets detailed requirements for prohibited articles, screening standards and staff responsibilities.

2.1 UK terrorism threat levels

Threat levels describe the likelihood of a terrorist attack:

Changes in Motivation, Intent or Capability can move the threat level up or down.

2.3 Types and locations of attack

Types of attack

  • MTA – Marauding terrorist attack
  • Sabotage
  • Cyberattack
  • Hijacking
Mnemonic: MSCH – “Most Serious Critical Hazards”.

Locations

  • On Airport – inside the airport perimeter and terminals.
  • Off Airport – approaches, roads, rail links and public areas around the airport.
  • On Airplane – in the aircraft cabin or hold.
Mnemonic: OOO – “On, Off, On”.

2.4 Device types and explosives

IED vs IID

TypeDescriptionComponentsBehaviour
IED Improvised Explosive Device Main charge, detonator, firing switch/timer, power source Explodes; requires detonation
IID Incendiary Improvised Device Container, initiation system, incendiary material, ignitor Burns; requires ignition

Explosive types

  • Homemade: e.g. TATP, HMTD – sensitive and powerful.
  • Military: e.g. C4, PETN.
  • Commercial: e.g. TNT, dynamite.

Device deployment platforms

  • PBIED – Person-borne IED.
  • VBIED – Vehicle-borne IED.
  • DBIED – Drone-borne IED.
  • IM – Improvised mortar.
  • HP/D – Hand placed or delivered IED / IID.
  • VAAW – Vehicle as a weapon.
3 Prohibited articles, firearms and components ▶

3.1 Prohibited in cabin and hold

Examples – cabin only

  • Scissors with blades 6 cm or more from the pivot.
  • Sharp blades with blade length of 6 cm or more.
  • Razor blades and many tools.
  • Liquids over permitted cabin volume.

Examples – prohibited in both cabin and hold

  • Undeclared firearms and ammunition.
  • Explosive materials, detonators and fuses.
  • Grenades and smoke grenades.
  • Fireworks and pyrotechnics (including many party poppers and crackers).

The legal basis is set out in the SCD and related regulations.

3.2 Firearms – definition and parts

Firearm definition:

  • Any device that allows the launching of a projectile (solid or liquid).
  • Treated as a firearm if it appears capable of doing so, even if it does not work.

Revolver/basic firearm components (RE)

Mnemonic: “Cool Metal Cylinder”.
  • Crane
  • Main frame
  • Cylinder

Self-loading firearm components

Mnemonic: “Big Metal Rifle Runs Magazines Cleanly & Tight”.
  • Barrel
  • Main frame
  • Return spring
  • Return spring guide
  • Magazine
  • Cross pin
  • Top slide

Ammunition components

Mnemonic: BCPP – “Bullet, Case, Primer, Powder”.
  • Projectile / bullet
  • Casing
  • Primer
  • Propellant (powder)

3.3 Suspicious concealment and handling prohibited items

Signs of concealment

  • Unusual location of dense items.
  • Abnormal weight or balance of bags.
  • Extra stitching or thick seams.
  • Additional zips or hidden compartments.

Handling prohibited but legal items (non-FA/EX)

  • Body search: inform SM discreetly; mark search incomplete if required by SOP.
  • Bag search: remove item, place out of reach, complete search, inform SM, offer options (return landside, left luggage, voluntary abandon).

Handling firearms or explosives (FA/EX)

  • Stop the search immediately.
  • Do not touch the item further.
  • Move bag and tray away if safe.
  • Ask customer to remain.
  • Discreetly inform SM and record search as incomplete.
  • Follow local emergency procedures.
4 X-ray, CT, EDS, CIP and TIP ▶

4.1 X-ray basics and RFX

RFX – Recognition of Firearms and Explosives – is the skill of identifying firearms, explosives and components on X-ray images.

X-ray colour coding

  • Organic materials (food, paper, many explosives): orange.
  • Inorganic (metals, ceramics): blue.
  • Mixed organic/inorganic (electronics, many IED mixes): green.

Density: brighter = higher density, lighter/paler = lower density.

Many homemade explosives can appear as green because they mix organic and inorganic components.

4.2 3D X-ray / CT – capabilities and limitations

Mnemonic: FISH – Fatigue, Image rotation, Size limit, Heavy objects.

Capabilities

  • View inside bags without opening them.
  • Reduces need for manual checks of every bag.
  • Provides 360° rotation and slice views.
  • Often allows electricals and liquids to stay in the bag.
  • Helps highlight possible explosives and prohibited articles.

Limitations

  • Subject to human error and operator fatigue.
  • Cannot screen items larger than the tray.
  • Operators have maximum continuous screen time and limited decision time per image (local policy).
  • X-rays can damage camera film.
  • Very dense objects can block or distort images.

CT equipment test bag

  • Looks like a normal case externally.
  • Image shows specific markers (e.g. a red and a yellow square).
  • Used by SM to test system and operator performance.
  • Must always be rejected and never treated as a normal bag.

4.3 Automated detection and alarms

EDS – Explosive Detection System

  • Automated system highlighting potential explosive threats (often red square).

Obvious Threat button

  • Yellow button at top of the screen.
  • Used only for obvious firearm or explosive device threats.

Shield/density alarm

  • System-generated alarm over very dense areas (yellow square).
  • Indicates manual bag search is needed.

Operator alarms (HSO)

  • Purple – initial marking of suspect area.
  • Pink – confirmation after operator analysis.

4.4 CIP and TIP

CIP – Centralised Image Processing

  • Images from different lanes are sent to a central pool.
  • Trained operators can screen remotely, balancing workload and throughput.

TIP – Threat Image Projection

  • Simulated threat images injected into real bag images to test operators.
  • Only one category active at a time: devices, firearms, sharps or other.

TIP pop-up meanings

  • Green – TIP image correctly detected.
  • Red – incorrect response or timeout.
  • Yellow – real image; log out and speak to SM.
5 Security behaviour and golden rules ▶

5.1 Security behaviour

  • Be alert to inappropriate questions.
  • Do not discuss security matters with people who do not need to know.
  • Do not post security information on social media.
  • Keep PINs, passwords, keys, ID and equipment secure; report compromises to SM immediately.
  • Keep uniform branding hidden outside work.
  • Return uniforms; do not throw them away.
  • Never misuse ID passes or access permissions.

5.2 Consequences of poor behaviour

  • Disciplinary action.
  • Loss of job.
  • Possible criminal charges.
  • Serious safety and security risks.
6 Flow-style “what to do when” ▶

6.1 Suspicious IED/IID image

  • Pause the image; use zoom, pan, slice, rotate.
  • If obvious firearm or IED → press Obvious Threat button.
  • If possible threat → mark with purple then pink alarm.
  • Reject bag and send for manual search.
  • Inform SM as per local procedures.

6.2 Shield/density alarm

  • Note yellow shield/density alarm over a dense area.
  • Reject the bag for manual search.
  • During search, check for dense or concealed items.

6.3 TIP pop-up

  • Green – continue working.
  • Red – performance issue; may need feedback/training.
  • Yellow – real image; log out and notify SM.
7 Checkpoint flow – loading area and greeting ▶

7.1 Loading area

  • Start position of the X-ray/3D check.
  • Everything must be inside the tray.
  • Observe passengers for hidden items and concealments.
  • Use profiling and conversation to assess behaviour.

Items that must not go through X-ray/3D

  • Old camera film.
  • Human organs – cannot go through X-ray; use ETD and call SM.
  • Pets.
  • Outside coats (to be removed).
  • Large metal items – manual bypass and notify SM.

7.2 Greeting and service

  • Smile and maintain eye contact.
  • Greet and clearly advise passengers.
  • Aim for excellent service; “security first, then customer service”.
  • Be aware of passengers’ contexts (funerals, ashes, holidays, transfers).
8 People screening – QPS, HHMD, SAMDEX, ETD ▶

8.1 Preparing passengers for QPS

Before QPS, ensure:

  • Coats, jackets, scarves, hats and camera glasses removed.
  • Shoes and boots above the ankle removed.
  • Belts, phones, pens and passports removed.
  • Pockets fully emptied.
  • Large belt pouches, textured jumpers, large hair accessories and large jewellery removed.
  • Religious wear can stay on (per SOP).

Rules: all passengers pass through QPS unless an exception applies; passenger should only pass through once – if body search starts, it must be completed.

QPS flow

  • Prepare passenger and remove required items.
  • Identify exceptions (wheelchair, infant, pacemaker, very tall).
  • If exception → adapted search (manual, HHMD, ETD).
  • If no exception → run QPS scan.
  • If clear → passenger continues.
  • If activation → follow QPS activation logic.

8.2 QPS zones, RND and exceptions

Zones

  • Zone 1 – head.
  • Zone 2 – shoulders, arms to waistband.
  • Zone 3 – waistband to knees.
  • Zone 4 – knees to feet.

Random (RND) activation

  • Always shows at left ankle.
  • Indicates random test – send to SAMDEX.
  • If RND plus real activation – ignore random marker, treat real zones as threats.

Scan issues

  • Inadequate scan → correct position, rescan once; if twice, use full body physical search.
  • Insufficient quality → ask passenger to step back, inform SM.

Non-medical refusal of QPS

  • Call SM.
  • Conduct search in private.
  • Remove shoes and X-ray them.
  • Perform full body physical search with HHMD and ETD.
  • Keep rejected bags in search lane.
  • Pregnancy is treated as non-medical for refusal and needs adapted handling.

QPS capabilities and limitations

CapabilitiesLimitations
No gender differentiationCannot scan customers under 1 m tall
Less physically demanding for customersAdapted search needed over 2 m height
Detects metallic and non-metallic itemsCustomer can refuse QPS

8.3 HHMD – hand held metal detector

Use HHMD:

  • For every physical body search unless medically exempt.
  • When physical search is restricted (plaster casts).
  • On areas highlighted by QPS or hard to search by hand.

When HHMD activates

  • Check ~6 inch circle around activation area with HHMD then physical search.
  • Note all activations and findings.

Capabilities and limitations

CapabilitiesLimitations
Can be used anywhereOnly detects metal
Instant detection of metalLimited penetration depth
Less intrusive than full body searchReacts to nearby metal structures
Simple and easy to useDependent on batteries
Visual and audible alarmVulnerable to human error

8.4 SAMDEX – shoe scanner

Use SAMDEX:

  • For any QPS RND activation.
  • For any zone 4 activation (except boots above ankle).

Combination rules

  • RND + no shoes → ETD only.
  • RND + shoes → SAMDEX only.
  • RND + real activations → physical 6-inch search + HHMD + SAMDEX.

SAMDEX colours

  • Green – clear.
  • Yellow – incomplete; repeat.
  • Yellow ×2 – treat as real; escalate to SM.
  • Blue – metallic activation; escalate to SM.
  • Orange – possible explosive; escalate to SM.

Escalation

  • If 2x yellow, or blue, or orange → shoes off → zone 4 physical search + HHMD → shoes X-rayed.

Exceptions

  • Medical condition preventing stance → remove shoes and X-ray; if cannot remove, ETD shoes/fastenings + physical search.
  • Infants and babies → no need to remove shoes for SAMDEX.
  • Wheelchair users → do not use SAMDEX; use X-ray and ETD.

Non-medical refusal

  • Call SM.
  • Private search; shoes removed and X-rayed.
  • Full physical search + HHMD + ETD.
  • Rejected bags remain in lane.
  • Pregnancy is not a medical reason for refusal.

SAMDEX capabilities and limitations

CapabilitiesLimitations
Often eliminates need to remove shoesOnly one foot scanned at a time
Detects metallic and explosive itemsNearby metal can cause misreadings
Provides instant resultsCannot be used by wheelchair users
Compact footprintHeavy and not easily moved

8.5 ETD – people

Purpose

  • Supports body search by detecting explosive particles on clothing, skin and shoes.

Technique

  • Buttons/fastenings – both sides of both palms, around buttons.
  • Shoes – across, around back and laces.
  • Waistband – if no shoes.
  • Religious headwear – “halo and mohawk” pattern.

Use ETD for

  • Random activations, especially when no shoes.
  • QPS activations on headwear.
  • No buttons → use hands; no shoes → use waistband.

Results

  • Green – no explosive detected; continue.
  • Red – repeat; if red twice, escalate to SM.
  • Yellow – SM check / local procedure.

Health and safety

  • Do not use bare hands; change gloves and check traps regularly.
  • Keep hot liquids away from the machine.
  • Use footstools correctly.
  • SM is responsible for checks and record-keeping.
9 LAGs, EMA-4 and bag ETD ▶

9.1 LAGs – liquids, aerosols and gels

  • Spreads – peanut butter, jam, cream, cheese, hair gel.
  • Sprays – deodorant, spray paint.
  • Liquids that pour, spill or drink.

9.2 EMA-4

Electromagnetic analyser for sealed and some unsealed LAGs, using non-ionising fields; container stays closed.

  • Main body, control panel, inspection compartment, ATOF probe.

Can be tested

  • Sealed bottles.
  • Sachets.
  • Tetra packs.
  • Pouches.
  • Factory-sealed cans with visible measurement.

Cannot be tested

  • Open cups or paper cups.
  • Cups or beakers with straws.
  • Plastic bags of liquid.
  • Refillable metal containers.
  • Flasks and leaking containers.

EMA-4 results

  • Green – cleared.
  • Red – not OK; keep in cavity and escalate to SM.
  • Yellow once – retest.
  • Yellow twice – test on another machine; if green there, clear; if yellow again, escalate to SM.

SM actions on LAG activation

  • Check procedure was followed correctly.
  • Question and profile the passenger.
  • Perform extra bag/body searches.
  • Use voluntary abandon or disposal if needed.
  • Decide if passenger continues or is escalated.
  • Complete LAG activation paperwork.

9.3 ETD – bag items

  • ETD any wrapped item.
  • ETD electrical/electronic items with batteries or power.
  • ETD human organ containers.
  • ETD camera films and cameras.
10 Bag search – process and safety ▶

10.1 When bag search is required

  • Legal requirement whenever the system demands it.
  • Equipment failure.
  • Alarm or activation resolution.
  • Escalations or profiling concerns.

10.2 Types of bag search

  • Full layered search – entire tray and contents searched.
  • Enhanced plus – only items of concern searched.
  • Machine issues → revert to full layered search.

10.3 Bag search flow

  • Ask:
    • “May I search your bag?”
    • “Do you have anything sharp?”
    • “Can you open all zips and compartments?”
  • Move cleared items to clear tray.
  • Keep concerned items in testing tray.
  • Apply special rules for powders (e.g. >350 g to USA, baby powder only with child under 24 months present).
  • On discovery of prohibited items, follow handling rules from Section 3.3.

10.4 Bag search – capabilities vs limitations

CapabilitiesLimitations
Acts as visual deterrentRequires consent
Can be done anywhereMay invade privacy or embarrass
Allows closer examinationTime-consuming
Uses sight, touch, hearing, smellCan be unpleasant or unhygienic
Allows profiling of customersVulnerable to human error; some items hard to search

10.5 Health and safety during bag search

  • Use manual handling aids like trolleys.
  • Test weight before lifting trays.
  • Avoid overreaching, twisting, or placing hands near sensors.
  • Use QR code on tray to rescan images if needed.
  • Ask the sharps question.
  • Ask passengers to open and show compartments.
  • Wear searching gloves – mandatory.
11 Special items and exemptions ▶

11.1 STEBs and sensitive items

STEBs (Security Tamper Evident Bags)

  • Clear plastic bags for LAGs purchased airside.
  • Must be sealed and have a receipt.
  • Placed in a separate tray and checked for tampering.

Cremated remains (ashes)

  • Can be X-rayed.
  • If image suggests >100 g or a prohibited article → reject, visual/physical inspection.

Human organs

  • Cannot go through X-ray.
  • Handled with ETD and SM involvement.

Camera film

  • Escalate to SM.
  • Conduct full hand checks.
  • Avoid X-ray where possible due to film damage.

Evidence bags

  • Can go through X-ray.
  • Must not be opened or searched.

Diplomatic bags

  • Once sealed and checked by SM, exempt from standard security checks.

11.2 Complex and non-complex items

Complex or oversized items

  • Examples: musical instruments, complex equipment.
  • Call SM; perform full hand checks (and X-ray if allowed).

Non-complex items

  • Walking aids, sticks, umbrellas, poster tubes – hand-search without SM.

Pushchairs and prams

  • Collapsible must fit trays; if not, go through QPS and full hand check.
  • Must be completely emptied and stored in a tray.
12 People categories, consent and authorities ▶

12.1 Age, consent and witnesses

  • Age of consent is 18 and over.
  • Under 18s must have a parent/guardian witnessing the entire search.
  • If guardian leaves → stop search and resume only when they return.
  • If no guardian → lane partner acts as witness.
  • Bring parent/guardian in first to witness.

12.2 Gender and identity

  • Male officers search male passengers.
  • Female officers search female passengers.
  • For transgender and non-binary passengers: follow SOP based on how they present and ensure respectful same-gender search.

12.3 Hidden disabilities and mobility

  • Sunflower lanyard indicates hidden disabilities – adapt communication and approach.

Wheelchair users

  • Shoes removed before body scan.
  • Adapted search often replaces standard QPS.

Medical aids

Splints, braces, casts, prosthetics follow colour-coded rules (visual inspection, ETD, removal, HHMD) as per training cards.

12.4 Compliance authorities and VIPs

  • Official items generally do not need removal.
  • If they must respond to an incident → stop search immediately.
  • Adapt searches while respecting authority; ask them to empty pockets and remove hats.
  • Vests and belts may be exempt.
  • Includes police, emergency services, escorts with detainees, VIPs, royals, dog handlers, K-card holders.
13 Tray management and lane health & safety ▶

13.1 Importance of tidy trays

  • Untidy trays cause delays and unnecessary rejections.
  • Make images harder to read; increase risk of breaches.
  • Cause jams, blockages and possible damage.

Good tray practice

  • One bag per tray where possible.
  • Lay items flat.
  • Use pause buttons when needed.
  • Avoid overhanging items.
  • Do not push trays or place into S-bends.
  • Secure loose and light items.
  • Do not overload trays.
  • Use clear voice instructions.

13.2 General lane safety

  • Do not put hands into rollers or over sensors.
  • Tie back hair; secure ties, scarves and lanyards.
  • Encourage passengers to collect items promptly.
  • Do not unplug equipment; watch for loose wires.
  • Do not lean on the QPS machine.
  • Clean spillages quickly; report hazards.
  • Use footstools safely and anti-fatigue mats where available.
  • Equipment testing is only done by SM.
